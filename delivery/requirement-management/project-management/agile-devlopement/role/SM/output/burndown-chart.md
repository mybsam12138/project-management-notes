# Burndown Chart & Progress Tracking

## 1. What is a Burndown Chart?

A Burndown Chart is a visual representation of:
- Remaining work
- Over time (daily within a sprint)

It helps the team understand:
- Whether they are on track
- If progress is stable or risky

---

## 2. Key Components

### X-Axis (Time)
- Sprint days (Day 1 → Day N)

### Y-Axis (Work Remaining)
- Story points OR hours

---

## 3. Two Lines

### Ideal Line
- Linear decrease from total work to zero

### Actual Line
- Real remaining work per day

---

## 4. Example

| Day | Remaining Work (Hours) |
|-----|----------------------|
| Day 1 | 40 |
| Day 2 | 36 |
| Day 3 | 30 |
| Day 4 | 28 |
| Day 5 | 20 |
| Day 6 | 18 |
| Day 7 | 10 |
| Day 8 | 6 |
| Day 9 | 3 |
| Day 10 | 0 |

---

## 5. How to Interpret

### Healthy Pattern
- Smooth downward trend
- Close to ideal line

### Warning Signs

#### Flat Line
- No progress
- Possible blockers

#### Sudden Drop at End
- Work not updated daily
- Poor task breakdown

#### Upward Movement
- Scope creep
- Underestimation

---

## 6. Daily Update Rule

Each day:
- Update remaining work
- Do NOT just mark tasks done at the end

---

## 7. Common Mistakes

### ❌ Tracking completed work instead of remaining work
→ Burndown must track remaining

### ❌ Updating only at sprint end
→ Loses visibility

### ❌ Tasks too large
→ No daily progress visible

---

## 8. Best Practices

- Break tasks into ≤ 1 day effort
- Update daily after standup
- Use hours or story points consistently
- Combine with impediment tracking

---

## 9. Relationship with Scrum Master

Scrum Master uses Burndown Chart to:
- Detect issues early
- Identify blockers
- Facilitate team discussion
- Improve sprint predictability

---

## 10. Summary

A Burndown Chart is:

> A simple but powerful tool to visualize progress and detect risks early during a sprint.
